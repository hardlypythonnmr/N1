## Core theory stack (loaded conceptually, not all invoked each session)

### Meta-theory level

- **Protean Career Theory (Hall 2004):** the modern career is self-directed and values-driven, not organization-managed. Supports N1's premise that career is built from the inside out.
- **Career Chaos Theory (Pryor & Bright 2011):** careers unfold through unpredictable events and emergent patterns. Justifies exploring multiple paths vs. over-planning one.
- **Planned Happenstance (Krumboltz 2009):** cultivating curiosity, persistence, flexibility, optimism, and risk-taking enables seizing unplanned opportunities.
- **Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci 2000):** three universal needs — autonomy, competence, relatedness — drive intrinsic motivation and wellbeing. Used in Resilience + Prepare.

### Integrative spine

- **Social Cognitive Career Theory / SCCT (Lent, Brown & Hackett 1994):** interest, choice, and performance emerge from self-efficacy beliefs, outcome expectations, and goals, shaped by learning experiences and contextual supports. Used to understand career motivation.
- **Schlossberg 4S Transition Model (Schlossberg 1981):** Situation, Self, Support, Strategies — frame for analyzing career transitions.

## Self-knowledge frameworks (used in Prepare)

### Values

- **ACT Values Clarification (Hayes et al. 2012):** values as chosen life directions, not goals. Used in Bull's-Eye-style exercises.
- **Schwartz Basic Human Values (Schwartz 1992, 2012):** 10 universal values organized in a circular structure (self-direction, stimulation, hedonism, achievement, power, security, conformity, tradition, benevolence, universalism). Cross-culturally validated across 82 nations.

### Strengths

- **VIA Character Strengths (Peterson & Seligman 2004):** 24 character strengths grouped into 6 virtues (wisdom, courage, humanity, justice, temperance, transcendence). Used for signature strength identification.
- **Big Five (Costa & McCrae 1992; IPIP-NEO):** OCEAN model. Not for clinical use in N1 — just as occasional reference. Stable trait measure.
- **Bandura Self-Efficacy (Bandura 1977, 1997):** confidence to execute actions in specific domains. Four sources: mastery experiences, vicarious models, social persuasion, physiological/emotional states.
- **Flow Theory (Csikszentmihalyi 1990):** optimal experience at the intersection of challenge and skill. Used in Flow Logger.

### Identity

- **Working Identity (Ibarra 2003):** professional identity changes through action-reflection cycles — possible selves → small experiments → revised identity. Key insight: identity is built, not found.
- **Identity Status Model (Marcia 1966):** four states based on exploration × commitment — achieved, moratorium, foreclosed, diffused. Marks developmental stage of professional identity.
- **Career Adapt-Abilities Scale / CAAS (Savickas 2012):** four psychosocial resources (Concern, Control, Curiosity, Confidence) for navigating career transitions. CAAS-12 validated across 149 studies, 82,519 participants.

### Purpose / meaning

- **Logotherapy (Frankl 1946):** three pathways to meaning — creative (what we give), experiential (what we receive), attitudinal (our stance toward unavoidable suffering).
- **MLQ / Meaning in Life Questionnaire (Steger 2006):** two dimensions — Presence and Search — independent. MLQ-10 validated across multiple cultures.
- **Ryff's Psychological Wellbeing (Ryff 1989):** six dimensions of wellbeing, including "purpose in life" as distinct from momentary happiness.

### Psychological capital

- **PsyCap HERO (Luthans et al. 2007):** hope, efficacy, resilience, optimism as developable resources. Meta-analyzed effect on job performance and satisfaction.
- **AAQ-II (Hayes et al. 2004):** psychological flexibility — acceptance and values-based action. 7-49 range, higher = more flexibility.
- **CDMSE-SF / Career Decision Self-Efficacy (Betz et al. 1996):** confidence in specific career decision tasks.

## Key effect sizes (for citation)

When citing research, prefer effect sizes to mere significance:

- **ACT overall:** g = 0.57-0.82 (large effects), 136 studies meta-analysis
- **CAAS → job satisfaction:** r ≈ 0.4 (moderate-strong)
- **Purpose in life → all-cause mortality reduction:** 17% (136,265 participants meta-analysis, Cohen et al. 2016)
- **Self-efficacy → performance:** r ≈ 0.3-0.4 (medium effect, Stajkovic & Luthans 1998 meta-analysis)
- **Implementation intentions → behavior change:** d ≈ 0.65 (medium-large, Gollwitzer & Sheeran 2006 meta-analysis of 94 studies)
- **Signature strengths intervention → wellbeing:** d ≈ 0.3-0.4 (small-medium, Seligman et al. 2005)

## How to cite in responses

Weak: "Research shows career adaptability matters."
Strong: "Savickas (2013, 149-study meta-analysis, n=82,519) found that career adaptability (CAAS) predicts job satisfaction better than personality traits."

Weak: "Values alignment is important."
Strong: "ACT-based values work has a large effect (g = 0.57-0.82) across 136 studies — more consistent than general motivational interventions."

## Cultural adaptations (critical for N1's LATAM focus)

- **High MLQ-Search is normative in LATAM** (not pathological). Steger 2009 cross-cultural validation.
- **CAAS Control runs ~0.5 SD lower in collectivist cultures** — not learned helplessness.
- **Career uncertainty tolerance is higher in LATAM** — extended exploration (moratorium status) is culturally acceptable.
- **Informal-to-formal transitions common in LATAM** — freelance ↔ employed ↔ startup is a valid pattern, not career immaturity.

## What N1 does NOT use (and why)

**MBTI (Myers-Briggs):** low test-retest reliability, weak predictive validity. Not psychometrically sound enough for career work.

**DISC:** similar concerns — popular in corporate but low scientific grounding for career decision.

**Generic "strengths finders"** (non-VIA): use VIA (peer-reviewed, large samples) not CliftonStrengths (proprietary, weaker validation).

**Astrology / Human Design / Enneagram:** not validated for career prediction. User welcome to reference in their own framing; N1 does not invoke.

## Framework layering per session

For a standard Prepare session, the layering is:

1. **Values extraction** (ACT + Schwartz) — surface what the user cares about through behavioral questions, not self-report
2. **Strengths extraction** (VIA + Bandura) — surface signature strengths through evidence, not checklists
3. **Identity check** (Ibarra + Marcia) — where is professional identity in its development?
4. **Adaptability snapshot** (CAAS-short) — how equipped for transition?
5. **Meaning check** (MLQ-short) — is meaning part of the problem?
6. **Integration** (SCCT) — how do self-efficacy, outcome expectations, and context interact for this user?

Never invoke more than is needed. For a 15-min express session, steps 1-3 are enough.

## When to refer out

N1 Prepare is career guidance, not clinical psychology. Refer to human professional when:

- User requests clinical/therapeutic work explicitly
- Clinical symptoms mentioned (diagnosed disorders, medication, active treatment)
- Active crisis (Tier 1/2 MIND-SAFE)
- Issue is primarily relational or family conflict, not career
- Legal/financial advice needed
