## Why speech matters in interviews

Content and delivery contribute separately to interview outcomes. Two candidates with identical content but different delivery have measurably different offer rates:

- Final Round AI 2026: 67% interview-success rate with structured speech drills vs. 23% with generic prep.
- Yoodli 2025 data: after 4-6 practice rounds with speech feedback, filler-word rate drops 60-80%.
- Bucholtz & Hall 2005 (linguistic anthropology): self-presentation register is trainable, not innate.

The interview is a performance. Not a fake performance — a **high-stakes conversation** where signal carries more than usual. Speech science helps the candidate signal intentionally.

## Filler words — taxonomy and thresholds

### Spanish fillers (Colombian / neutral LATAM register)

| Filler | Common use | Replace with |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| "eh" / "ehm" | Buying time mid-thought | Pause (silence is fine — 1-2 seconds) |
| "este" | Searching for next word | Pause + continue |
| "o sea" | Self-correction | "Es decir" or restructure |
| "como que" | Softening | Remove entirely |
| "tipo" | Vague categorization | Replace with specific example |
| "básicamente" | Simplifying | Often removable |
| "literal" / "literalmente" | Over-used Gen-Z filler | Remove; keep only when literally meaning literal |
| "es que..." | Starting defensively | Remove opener, state directly |

### English fillers

| Filler | Common use | Replace with |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| "um" / "uh" | Buying time | Pause |
| "like" | Vague approximation | Replace with specific or remove |
| "you know" | Seeking confirmation | Remove |
| "basically" | Simplifying | Often removable |
| "literally" | Amplification | Remove unless literal |
| "I mean..." | Self-correction opener | Replace or remove |
| "so, yeah..." | Ending weakly | End with your Result, drop this |

### Threshold (per 60 seconds)

- **0-2 fillers / 60s:** excellent — conversational, confident
- **3-7 fillers / 60s:** normal range — human, not over-polished
- **8-14 fillers / 60s:** noticeable — train to reduce
- **15+ fillers / 60s:** distracting — interviewer notices, content gets obscured

**Important:** Zero fillers sounds robotic. The goal is 2-5 per 60-second answer. Natural, not scripted.

## Pacing (WPM)

Words-per-minute is measurable from transcription: count words, divide by seconds, multiply by 60.

| WPM | Effect |
|-----|--------|
| <110 | Too slow — interviewer's mind wanders; sounds uncertain or rumiante |
| 110-130 | Reflective pace — OK for complex technical answers, too slow for rapport questions |
| 130-160 | **Conversational sweet spot** — clear, confident, natural |
| 160-190 | Fast — OK if content is familiar; can signal enthusiasm or anxiety |
| 190+ | Too fast — interviewer loses details; strong anxiety signal |

**Rule:** vary your WPM depending on answer type.
- **Behavioral/story answers:** 130-155 WPM (conversational, room for emphasis)
- **Technical deep-dives:** 115-140 WPM (slower, more precision)
- **Rapport / small talk:** 145-165 WPM (energetic)

### How to slow down

Speakers who run too fast often:
- Don't breathe between sentences — insert breath pauses
- Fear silence — they pack sentences to avoid it
- Have rehearsed phrases without internalizing → they rush through

Drill: read the STAR structure out loud, pause 1 full second at each capital letter (S · T · A · R). This builds natural pacing rhythm.

## Prosody — pitch contour

Prosody = how pitch moves through a sentence.

### Pattern 1 — Terminal fall (confidence signal)

In declarative sentences, pitch drops at the end:
```
"Migramos en 10 semanas ↘"     (fall at the end = certainty)
```

### Pattern 2 — Upward inflection / uptalk (uncertainty signal)

Rising pitch at end of declarative sentences (common in tentative speakers):
```
"Migramos en 10 semanas ↗?"   (rise at the end = asking for approval)
```

Uptalk is interpreted as **low confidence** by interviewers, especially in technical contexts. Train to end declaratives with falling pitch.

### Pattern 3 — Flat / monotone (disengagement signal)

No pitch variation:
```
"Migramos en 10 semanas —"     (flat = boredom or recited)
```

Interpreted as **disinterest** or **rehearsed**. Fix: invest pitch range into key words — the ones carrying the meaning. Rise slightly on the skill you emphasize, fall on the outcome.

### How to check prosody from transcription

Pure text can't capture prosody directly. But you can analyze:
- **Question marks where they shouldn't be** — if the transcription adds them, you might be uptalking
- **Sentence variety** — if every sentence has the same length, the rhythm is monotone
- **Listener feedback** — ask a friend: "¿Sonaba seguro o suena como que preguntaba?"

## Hedging density

Hedges are words that weaken assertions:

| Spanish hedge | Replace with |
|---------------|--------------|
| "Yo creo que..." | "Encontré que..." or remove ("yo creo" is implicit) |
| "Tal vez" | "Probablemente" (if actually probable) or remove |
| "No sé si..." | "No estoy seguro de..." or address the uncertainty directly |
| "Como que..." | Remove, replace with precise term |
| "Medio / algo así como" | Remove |
| "Para ser honesto" | Remove — honesty should be default |

### Threshold

- **< 1 hedge per 40-50 words:** normal, natural
- **1-2 hedges per 40-50 words:** moderate — consider tightening
- **3+ hedges per 40-50 words:** weakening your content — rewrite

### Example rewrite

Weak:
> "Yo creo que tal vez el resultado fue bueno, como que duplicamos retención, pero no sé, tal vez tuvo que ver con otros factores también."
(5 hedges in one sentence.)

Strong:
> "El resultado: duplicamos retención. Parte del crédito fue del rediseño, parte de otros factores de contexto que no controlo."
(Acknowledges uncertainty without hedging the result.)

## Strong verbs (action forward)

Passive and weak verbs dilute impact. Default to active, specific.

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "I was responsible for..." | "I led...", "I built...", "I owned..." |
| "I worked on..." | "I shipped...", "I designed...", "I launched..." |
| "I helped with..." | "I contributed by...", "I partnered to..." |
| "I participated in..." | "I led the [specific part]..." |
| "I was involved in..." | "I drove...", "I managed...", "I executed..." |

If you genuinely contributed peripherally (not led), say so honestly — "I supported the rollout by X" — don't inflate. Interviewers detect inflation fast.

## The 60-second structure

Target answer length for behavioral questions: **60-90 seconds**. Longer answers lose listener attention; shorter answers feel incomplete.

Internal structure:

- **Hook (8-12 seconds):** anchors the listener in a specific situation. One sentence.
- **Situation (10-15 seconds):** context, just enough so a stranger can follow.
- **Task (8-12 seconds):** what was yours to do, why it mattered.
- **Action (20-30 seconds):** the bulk — what you did, how, why. Specific choices, named tradeoffs.
- **Result (10-15 seconds):** the outcome, ideally with a metric.
- **Learning (5-10 seconds, optional):** one non-obvious insight.

Keep it to 60-90 seconds. If the interviewer wants more, they'll ask.

## Drilling protocol

For serious improvement, the loop is:

1. **Pick one pattern** to focus on — e.g., filler word reduction.
2. **Record yourself** answering 3-4 questions.
3. **Transcribe** (Otter.ai / Whisper / Google Recorder).
4. **Score**: count fillers, measure WPM, find hedges.
5. **Rewrite** one answer, tightening everything.
6. **Record the rewritten version**.
7. **Compare** — did metrics improve?
8. **Repeat** 4-6 times over 2 weeks.

Daily 15-20 min drills beat 3-hour session-day cram. Yoodli and Orai research both confirm this.

## Culturally-specific notes (LATAM)

- **Colombian speakers:** "eh" and "o sea" are pervasive, rarely noticed by Colombian ears; noticed by US/European interviewers. When interviewing with US company, prioritize reduction of these.
- **Formality gradient:** Colombian professional culture expects warmth in tone. Don't over-polish to cold. Keep human register; just reduce literal filler words.
- **Bilingual interviews:** if switching between ES and EN, watch for filler-word bleed (using Spanish "este" in English answer). Practice each language's interview in that language.
