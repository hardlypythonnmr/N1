> Reference loaded by `skills/negotiate/SKILL.md`.
> Notes on LATAM-specific realities that affect negotiation. Not a salary database — a calibration layer.

---

## Why LATAM deserves its own reference

Negotiation advice written for US markets often fails or backfires in LATAM contexts:
- Salary transparency is structurally lower (opacity is norm, not signal)
- Relational warmth is expected; pure-transactional tone reads as cold/aggressive
- Employment structures differ (cesantías, primas, integral salary, AFP)
- Currency complexity (local, USD-fixed, USD-quoted-local-paid)
- Referral culture stronger; cold applications weaker
- Tax complexity for remote-to-US employment

## Country-specific notes

### Colombia

**Standard structure (non-integral):**
- Base salary monthly
- Cesantías: ~1 extra month/year, savings for unemployment; employer deposits
- Intereses de cesantías: ~12% of cesantías, paid Feb
- Prima: ~1 extra month/year split in June and December
- Vacation: 15 working days, paid
- ARL (workplace insurance): employer pays
- EPS (health insurance): ~4% employee, ~8.5% employer
- AFP (pension): ~4% employee, ~12% employer

Total cost to employer ≈ base × 1.52 (approx. "factor prestacional").

**Salario integral:**
- If base > 13 SMLMV (~15.2M COP in 2026), can opt for integral
- All-inclusive (no separate cesantías, primas, etc.)
- Factor usually 1.30 (25-30% premium vs base-only pay)
- If an offer is quoted as "integral", compare against standard to verify fairness

**Common negotiation levers in Colombia:**
- Moving from standard to integral (if earning > threshold) — usually neutral
- Home-office stipend
- Extra vacation days (rarely contested)
- Learning/conference budget
- Private insurance (pre-paid medicine) upgrades

### México

**Standard structure:**
- Base monthly
- Aguinaldo: 15 days minimum (most companies 30 days, premium ones 60+)
- Vacation: starts at 6 days/year, increases with tenure (but many companies offer 15+ from start)
- Prima vacacional: 25% of vacation days, paid at vacation start
- IMSS (social security): employee ~3%, employer varies
- INFONAVIT (housing): employer 5%
- AFORE (pension): employee 1.125%, employer 2%

**Common negotiation levers in Mexico:**
- Vales de despensa (food vouchers — tax advantageous)
- Seguro de gastos médicos mayores (major medical)
- Annual bonus
- Car/transportation allowance (if role requires)
- Home office stipend

### Argentina

**Context:**
- High inflation + currency volatility make pesos-only pay risky
- Many tech workers seek USD-fixed pay (via intermediaries like Deel, Remote.com)
- "Blue dollar" and regulation affects take-home for USD earners
- Severance laws (Ley de Contrato de Trabajo) strong — severance calculation protects employee

**Common negotiation levers:**
- USD fixed (most critical ask in 2026 Argentina context)
- Pago en criptomonedas or via international accounts (complex tax/legal)
- Vacation days above minimum
- Mental health coverage

### Brazil

**Standard structure:**
- 13th salary (legally mandatory, paid in December)
- INSS (social security), FGTS (severance fund) as employer burdens
- Vale refeição / vale alimentação (meal/food vouchers)

**Common negotiation levers:**
- Plano de saúde (health plan) quality
- Plano odontológico (dental)
- Vale refeição amount
- Auxílio home office
- Bonus PLR (participação nos lucros)

### Chile

- AFP (pension) mandatory, employee-paid ~10% of salary
- Isapre (private health) vs Fonasa (public) — many employees want Isapre coverage subsidized
- Aguinaldos (Fiestas Patrias, Christmas)
- Vacation: 15 working days

### Uruguay / Peru / Ecuador

Smaller markets, less data. Use regional benchmarks cautiously; local conversations with people in target role are often more reliable than aggregators.

## Currency pay mechanism — critical factor

### USD fixed (gold standard for LATAM tech workers)

Employer pays in USD, deposits to international account or crypto. Protects employee from FX volatility.

Common with:
- US-based companies (direct or via EOR)
- Crypto companies
- Some LATAM startups with international funding

### USD quoted, local paid

Employer says "$3,000 USD/mes" but pays the local currency equivalent at month's exchange rate. Employee eats FX volatility.

**Red flag:** common in LATAM outsourcing firms. If dollar weakens vs. local currency, employee's real purchasing power in USD drops. Negotiate: add FX-adjustment clause (re-set USD-quoted rate monthly or quarterly based on published rate).

### Local currency only

Most LATAM-local employers pay only in local currency. Fine if you live and spend locally. Problematic if you have USD obligations (travel, USD-priced software, savings in USD, family abroad).

### Mixed (base local + bonus USD, or vice versa)

Increasingly common at LATAM-US hybrid companies. Clarify exactly which components are which currency.

## Warmth in the negotiation — cultural calibration

US negotiation guides emphasize assertiveness, directness, and quick data-anchoring. This translates poorly to LATAM professional contexts.

**LATAM expectations:**
- Warm opening (acknowledge the offer, the team, the process)
- Brief personal framing (why this role matters)
- Data-anchored request delivered respectfully
- Close with reaffirmation of enthusiasm

**Anti-patterns for LATAM (avoid):**
- Leading with numbers (feels cold)
- Explicit ultimatums ("I need X or I walk")
- Pressure tactics ("I have 24 hours")
- Overly tactical framing (sounds like negotiation tactics book)

**Pro-patterns:**
- Personal warmth framed as genuine (because it should be)
- Explicit respect for internal processes
- Data delivered as context, not demand
- Alternatives offered (shows flexibility)
- Relationship-focused closing

## Equity in LATAM

Equity is rare in LATAM-local employment but common in US-based remote employment.

**Realities:**
- Most LATAM-local startups don't formally grant equity (verbal promises are common but legally weak)
- US-based companies hiring LATAM talent often do offer equity, sometimes via ESOP structures
- Tax treatment of equity for LATAM employees is complex (Colombia, Mexico, Argentina each different)
- Vesting: 4-year cliff 1-year is standard in US; LATAM local grants often shorter or less formalized

**When evaluating LATAM equity offer:**
- Is it documented in a legal instrument (not just a verbal promise)?
- What stage is the company? (pre-PMF = near-zero EV for most)
- What's the vesting? Cliff? Acceleration on exit?
- What's the dilution expected across future rounds?
- Tax treatment in your jurisdiction?

**Negotiation:** often LATAM candidates under-value equity in local startups (right call) and over-value equity in US companies (under-appreciate the vesting risk). Calibrate realistically.

## Referral premium

In LATAM, internal referral is worth roughly **2× callback rate** (per LinkedIn 2026 data) vs cold application.

Negotiation implication: if you got the role via referral, your leverage is slightly higher (because recruiting cost for the company was lower and trust is pre-built). Reasonable to ask for +5-10% over what cold-applied candidates would get.

## Visa / geographic complications

If offer is for:

- **Fully remote LATAM-based:** no visa issues, but consider payroll structure (local employment vs contractor)
- **Hybrid with travel required to US/EU:** confirm visa support + travel budget
- **Relocation:** negotiate relocation package explicitly (usually $5-15K for LATAM → US moves; more for family)
- **Regional role requiring travel within LATAM:** daily/weekly per diem, not just airfare

## The walk-away calibration for LATAM

Walk-away from an offer is harder in LATAM than US for these reasons:
- Smaller markets = fewer competing offers, longer to find new ones
- Runway may be thinner (lower savings rates in many LATAM countries)
- Social pressure from family to "take what's available"

**Honest calibration:** walk-away is viable when:
- You have 6+ months runway
- You have another active interview in late stages
- The offer is clearly bad (below P25 with red flags)
- The role is a significant step backward

Walk-away is NOT viable when:
- Runway < 3 months
- No other active processes
- Offer is middling but functional

**When leverage is low, negotiate modestly.** Don't walk away from a functional offer on principle. Practical > ideological.
