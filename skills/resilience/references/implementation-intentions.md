# Implementation Intentions — Gollwitzer's "if-then" scaffolding

> Reference loaded by `skills/resilience/SKILL.md`.
> Implementation intentions close the gap between intention and behavior. Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) meta-analysis of 94 studies: d = 0.65 (medium-large effect) on behavior change, especially for aversive or complex tasks.

---

## The core format

Not: "I will apply more this week."
Yes: **"If [specific cue], then [specific action]."**

The cue must be:
- **Time-anchored:** "Tuesday at 9am" or "right after lunch" or "when I sit down with coffee"
- **Place-anchored:** "at my desk" or "in my kitchen"
- **Context-anchored:** "after I open LinkedIn" or "before I open Netflix"

The action must be:
- **Specific:** not "work on my search" but "tailor 2 applications"
- **Bounded in time:** "for 45 minutes" (timer) or "until I submit 2"
- **Within reach:** achievable given current state

---

## Examples — weak vs strong

**Weak (goal intention):**
- "I'll apply more this week."
- "I should work on my CV."
- "I need to practice interviews."

**Strong (implementation intention):**
- "If it's Tuesday 9am and I'm at my desk with coffee, then I tailor 2 applications for 45 minutes."
- "If I open LinkedIn, then I first check for recruiter messages, respond if any, then close LinkedIn within 10 minutes."
- "If it's Thursday 7pm and I've finished dinner, then I practice 3 STAR answers out loud for 20 minutes."

---

## Why it works (mechanism)

Gollwitzer's theory: intentions are stored in memory and activated when cues appear. Without explicit cue-action pairing, intention activation depends on conscious recall — which fails under stress, distraction, or emotional load.

Implementation intentions **automate the transition from intention to action** by pre-binding cue and action. When the cue appears, action is triggered without requiring motivation in that moment.

Especially powerful for:
- Aversive tasks (where willpower depletes)
- Complex sequences (many decision points)
- Long-term goals (where motivation fluctuates)

Job search hits all three.

## Protocol for building one with a user

### Step 1 — Identify the target behavior

Ask: "What's the ONE thing you committed to doing this week that's most at risk of not happening?"

Common answers:
- Tailoring applications (not just submitting)
- Following up with recruiters
- Practicing for an interview
- Reaching out to one person
- Working on a portfolio piece

### Step 2 — Find a stable cue

The cue should be something that already happens reliably in the user's week. Options:

- **Time cue:** morning coffee, Sunday night, end of workday
- **Place cue:** kitchen table, commute, after gym
- **Event cue:** after kids sleep, after lunch, after a specific meeting

Avoid cues that require willpower to create. "Every morning at 6am" only works if the user already wakes at 6am.

### Step 3 — Bound the action

The action must be **do-able in 15-60 minutes**. Longer = doesn't start. Shorter = doesn't matter.

Include:
- **Start signal:** timer, location, tool open
- **Minimum completion:** "2 applications" or "20 minutes, whichever comes first"
- **End signal:** close the tool, move to next activity

### Step 4 — Name it aloud

Ask the user to say the full intention out loud:
"If it's [cue], then I [action]."

Have them repeat it 3 times. Research shows verbal rehearsal strengthens the cue-action binding.

### Step 5 — Pre-commit with accountability (optional but powerful)

One-line message to someone ("I'm committing to [intention]. I'll tell you Friday if I did it.") adds relatedness satisfaction.

## Advanced patterns

### Pattern 1 — Temptation bundling
Pair an aversive task with a reward.

- "If I'm tailoring an application, then I can listen to [favorite podcast / album]."
- "If I'm at the coffee shop, I can drink my favorite specialty coffee ONLY while working on applications."

Research: reduces resistance to aversive tasks by 40-50% in short-term studies.

### Pattern 2 — Obstacle anticipation (Gollwitzer's MCII / WOOP)
After setting the intention, anticipate the obstacle:

"If it's Tuesday 9am and I'm at my desk, then I tailor 2 applications for 45 minutes."
"But if I start feeling overwhelmed / start checking Twitter / feel like skipping, then I [specific recovery action, e.g., take 5 deep breaths and open just the first application]."

This pre-commitment to obstacle handling dramatically improves success rate.

### Pattern 3 — Streak leverage
Track consecutive days of intention-following. Once 5+ days, motivation shifts from willpower to identity ("I'm someone who does this").

Simple tracking: physical calendar, X marks each day the intention was honored. Visible streak = psychological cost of breaking it.

## Common failure modes

### Too ambitious
"If it's 9am, then I work for 4 hours on applications."
4 hours is too long. User starts, peters out, defines intention as "failed" and abandons.
**Fix:** start with 30-45 min max.

### Too vague
"If I feel ready, then I'll work on the search."
"Feeling ready" isn't a cue — it's an internal state that may never arrive.
**Fix:** external, observable cue (time + place).

### Outside user's control
"If my roommate stops playing music, then I practice interviews."
Depends on another person. Fragile.
**Fix:** user-controlled cue.

### No minimum completion
"If it's 9am, then I work on my search."
Without a minimum, user might open Notion for 2 minutes and check "done."
**Fix:** always bind a concrete minimum.

### Contradicts existing habit
"If I wake up, then I immediately start applications."
User has a strong habit of coffee + news first. Intention fights it and loses.
**Fix:** attach to existing habit, not replace it. "If I've had my coffee and seen the headlines, then I..."

Si es [cue específico con día + hora + lugar],
entonces [acción específica con duración/mínimo].

**Ejemplo construido con vos:**

**El próximo viernes, me decís:**
- Martes: sí / no
- Jueves: sí / no

Eso es todo. Sin gris. Binario. De ahí tomamos la decisión siguiente.

## When implementation intentions are NOT the right tool

- **Burnout colapsado:** user can't follow through because exhausted; needs rest first
- **Depression:** if depressed, intention-following requires energy that's absent; treat the depression before relying on intentions
- **Goal unclear:** if user doesn't know what they're trying to do, no intention will work. Go to Prepare first.
- **Crisis-level stress:** acute stress shrinks working memory; intentions may not activate. Lower the stakes first.
