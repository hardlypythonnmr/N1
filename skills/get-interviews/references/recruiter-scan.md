# Recruiter 6-8 Second Scan — How humans actually read CVs

> Reference loaded by `skills/get-interviews/SKILL.md`.
> Source: ResumeHeatMap 2026 eye-tracking study (n=2,200 recruiters across LATAM + US + EU).

---

## The scan order (6-8 seconds)

Eye-tracking data shows recruiters hit the following in this order, with typical time budget:

| Order | Element | Time | Question the recruiter is answering |
|-------|---------|------|-------------------------------------|
| 1 | Name + current title | ~0.5s | "Am I in the ballpark for this role?" |
| 2 | Years of experience | ~0.5s | "Seniority match?" |
| 3 | Last 2-3 job titles + companies | ~2s | "Is the career coherent?" |
| 4 | Outcomes/metrics in bullets | ~2-3s | "Is there proof of impact?" |
| 5 | Technical skills section | ~1s | "Visual keyword check" |
| 6 | Education | ~0.5-1s | "Only if items 1-5 unclear" |

**Total: 6-8 seconds.** If the CV fails the first few checkpoints, the recruiter doesn't continue to the details. The first ~4 seconds decide ~80% of outcomes.

---

## What recruiters WEIGHT (keep)

1. **Outcomes with metrics** — numbers, percentages, scale
   - Strong: "Grew organic traffic 47% YoY (from 120K to 176K monthly visits)"
   - Weak: "Worked on SEO initiatives to improve traffic"
2. **Title continuity** — career that "makes sense" (promotions, lateral moves explainable, titles in same function)
3. **Company recognition** — brand names matter in LATAM (Rappi, Mercado Libre, Nubank, Globant, Bancolombia as positive signals)
4. **Recent relevance** — last 2 roles weigh more than the first 2
5. **Technical skills match** — exact keywords from the JD visible in the skills block

---

## What recruiters IGNORE (or hate)

1. **Objective statements / summaries** — 80% skip. Exception: strong personal brand statement with specifics (rare).
2. **Soft skills in prose** — "collaborative team player with excellent communication skills" — universally skipped.
3. **Interests/hobbies** — ignored unless directly relevant ("competitive marathon runner" when applying to a sports brand; otherwise skip).
4. **LinkedIn endorsements count** — not on CV.
5. **References** — "Available upon request" is wasted space.
6. **Long paragraphs** — recruiters don't read them. Bullets or nothing.

## The bullet formula

Every experience bullet should answer: **What did you do · How · With what result.**

Pattern:
```
[Strong verb] [specific what] [optional how] [result with metric]
```

Examples:

**Strong:**
- "Led migration of 12-person data team from Redshift to Snowflake, reducing query costs by 38% and onboarding time for new analysts from 4 weeks to 5 days."
- "Built A/B testing framework in Python + SQL; ran 18 experiments in 6 months, driving $420K incremental revenue."
- "Redesigned checkout flow based on funnel analysis; lifted conversion from 2.1% to 3.4% (+62% relative).

**Weak:**
- "Responsible for migrating the data team to a new platform."
- "Worked on A/B testing and experimentation."
- "Improved the checkout experience."

**Verb choices (strong, action-forward):**
Led · Built · Launched · Migrated · Designed · Shipped · Grew · Reduced · Automated · Consolidated · Negotiated · Advocated · Coached · Recovered · Scaled · Rebuilt · Introduced · Prototyped · Validated · Quantified.

**Verbs to avoid (weak, passive):**
Responsible for · Assisted with · Involved in · Worked on · Participated in · Helped · Supported · Tasked with.

## The 2-column trap (mentioned but worth its own note)

Designers love 2-column CVs. Recruiters don't hate them — but ATS sometimes mangle them. The deeper issue: eye-tracking shows **people read the left column first, top-to-bottom, then the right column**. Putting your experience on the left and "contact info / skills" on the right can work, but putting them in reverse (skills left, experience right) means the recruiter reads skills first — and without context, may dismiss.

**Rule of thumb:** single column is safest. If you want two columns for design, put **experience and outcomes on the LEFT**.

## Optimal CV length

- **0-5 years experience:** 1 page.
- **5-12 years experience:** 1-2 pages. 2 pages is fine for senior roles.
- **12+ years experience (director/VP/C-suite):** 2 pages. Rarely 3.

**Never:** 3+ pages for non-executive roles. Recruiters stop reading.

**Whitespace matters.** A packed 1-page CV is harder to scan than a clean 2-page one. If the 1-page is cramped, go to 2.

## Country-specific adjustments

- **Colombia / México / Argentina:** photos common, often expected. Neutral professional headshot, passport-style. Not required for tech roles but not penalized.
- **Brazil:** photos common. Spanish/Portuguese dual CVs sometimes expected.
- **US / Canada:** no photo (can trigger bias filters). No age, no marital status.
- **Spain / Europe:** Europass template sometimes used. Photos common but declining.

For international applications: follow the destination country's norms.

## The "6-second test" — how to run it yourself

Show your CV to someone for exactly 6 seconds (cover with paper, reveal, cover again). Ask them:

1. What's my current role?
2. How many years of experience do I have?
3. What was my most recent job?
4. Name one outcome I delivered.
5. What technical skills do I have?

If they can't answer 4 of 5, the CV needs restructuring. The recruiter won't do better than your friend did in 6 seconds.

## When to escalate to the skill level

If the dual-audit score (ATS + scan) is low, but the user's background is genuinely strong, the problem might not be the CV. Check:

- **Title mismatch** (addressable via `skill-to-title-translation.md`)
- **Role-market fit** (applying to roles where the market is frozen — addressable via ghost-job analysis)
- **Geographic/remote fit** (applying outside where the company really hires)
- **Overqualification** (senior applying to mid-level — recruiters often filter out expecting flight risk)

The CV isn't always the problem. The audit should flag when something else is.
