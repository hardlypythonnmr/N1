# Stage 1: CV/Resume — Why People Fail Here

> Research compilation on failure modes at the CV/Resume screening stage of hiring processes.
> Last updated: 2026-03-10

---

## Key Findings Summary

1. **~97% of resumes submitted to online job postings are rejected** — only 2-3% result in an interview callback (CareerSidekick; Glassdoor).
2. **Recruiter initial scan takes 7.4 seconds** — eye-tracking shows attention goes to current title, previous title, dates, then education (TheLadders, 2018).
3. **The "75% ATS auto-rejection" claim is largely myth** — 92% of recruiters say their ATS does NOT auto-reject resumes (Enhancv, 2025). The real bottleneck is human volume management, not algorithmic rejection.
4. **Name-based racial discrimination remains persistent** — white-sounding names receive 36% more callbacks than Black names and 24% more than Latino names, with NO improvement since 1989 (Quillian et al., 2017 meta-analysis; n=55,842).
5. **18-27% of job postings are "ghost jobs"** with no real intent to hire (Greenhouse, 2024; LinkedIn analysis), meaning many rejections are structural rather than merit-based.
6. **Tailored resumes get 40-50% more callbacks** than generic ones (ResumeGo). Only 26% of resumes include quantified achievements (Cultivated Culture, n=125,000+).
7. **AI screening tools introduce new biases** — LLMs favor white-associated names 85% of the time and never favor Black male names over white male names (University of Washington, 2024).
8. **Geographic norms vary dramatically** — photos expected in LATAM/Europe but discouraged in US; personal data (age, marital status) standard in LATAM but illegal to request in the US.

## 2. ATS (Applicant Tracking Systems)

### Prevalence

- **97.8% of Fortune 500 companies** (489/500) use a detectable ATS (Jobscan, 2025).
- **94% of recruiters** say ATS has had a positive impact on their hiring processes.
- By 2025, **88% of companies** employ some form of AI for initial candidate screening.

### The "75% Auto-Rejection" Myth — Debunked

The widely cited claim that "75% of resumes are never read by a human" traces back to a defunct company and has been professionally debunked:

- **Enhancv 2025 study:** 92% of recruiters (23 out of 25) confirmed their ATS does NOT auto-reject resumes based on formatting, design, or content.
- Only **8% (2/25) of recruiters** said their ATS is configured to automatically reject resumes based on content or match scores.
- ATS platforms **organize, sort, and filter** — they do not autonomously reject candidates. The culprit behind "ghosted" applications is sheer volume.

**Source:** Enhancv recruiter study, 2025; HR.com analysis, 2025.
**Methodology:** Direct recruiter surveys + ATS configuration analysis.
**Confidence level:** Moderate-to-strong. The debunking evidence is recent (2025) and comes from multiple independent sources, but sample sizes in recruiter surveys are small (n=25 in the Enhancv study).

### What ATS Actually Does

- Parses and stores resumes in searchable databases.
- Allows recruiters to filter by keywords, experience level, location, education.
- Ranks candidates based on keyword match scores.
- Flags missing required qualifications.

### Factors That Cause ATS Parsing Failures

- Non-standard formatting (tables, columns, text boxes, headers/footers).
- Image-based PDFs or scanned documents.
- Creative fonts or graphics that can't be parsed.
- Missing standard section headers (Education, Experience, Skills).

**Key insight:** The real problem is not automatic rejection — it is that with 180-250 applicants per posting, recruiters use ATS filters to surface the top 10-20 candidates and may never scroll beyond that. Resumes that don't appear in filtered results are functionally invisible.

## 3. Recruiter Behavior

### Eye-Tracking Studies

#### TheLadders Eye-Tracking Study (2018)

- **Sample:** Professional recruiters tracked with eye-tracking technology.
- **Finding:** Recruiters spend an average of **7.4 seconds** on initial resume screen.
- **Previous finding (2012):** Average was 6 seconds during recession-era high-volume hiring.
- **Scan pattern:** Current title/company → Previous title/company → Dates of employment → Education.
- **Methodology:** Two-stage process — speed assessment first, then eye-tracking for attention patterns.
- **Publication:** TheLadders, 2018.

**Confidence level:** Moderate. The study is widely cited but the sample size of recruiters was not large, and the methodology of the original 2012 study has been questioned.

#### What Recruiters Focus On

| Resume Element | % of Screening Time | Source |
|----------------|---------------------|--------|
| Work experience section | 67% | Recruiter surveys |
| Job titles and subheadings | First scan target | TheLadders eye-tracking |
| Left-side navigation | Primary scan path | TheLadders |
| First few words of each bullet | Where eyes go after titles | TheLadders |
| Education | Bottom scan (quick check) | TheLadders |

### Time Spent per Resume

| Statistic | Value | Source |

### Recruiter Preferences

- **83%** say they're more likely to hire a candidate who tailored their resume to the specific job.
- **94%** say cover letter presence and quality influence interview decisions.
- Resumes with **simple layouts, clear sections, and heading titles** captured more recruiter attention.

**Confidence level:** Moderate. Eye-tracking data comes from small samples but is consistent across multiple studies.

### Mistakes That Trigger Rejection

### Resume Length

**ResumeGo Study (n=7,712 resumes):**

- Two-page resumes received **2.3x more callbacks** than one-page versions for candidates with 10+ years of experience.
- 2024 follow-up: two-page resumes had a **2.9% higher callback rate** for mid-to-senior candidates.
- **70% of hiring managers** prefer two-page resumes for candidates with significant experience.
- **475-600 words** produces an 8.2% interview rate (nearly double average).
- Resumes over 600 words see a **43% drop** in callbacks.

**Guideline by experience level:**
| Experience | Optimal Length |
|------------|---------------|
| 0-7 years | 1 page |
| 8-15 years | 1-2 pages |
| 15+ years / Executive | 2 pages |

**Confidence level:** Strong for the length finding. ResumeGo study had a large sample and controlled methodology.

### Quantified Achievements

**Cultivated Culture Analysis (n=125,000+ resumes):**

- Only **26% of resumes** included at least 5 instances of measurable results/metrics.
- **51% of resumes** included fluffy buzzwords, cliches, or incorrect pronoun use.
- Candidates only include **51% of relevant keywords** in their resumes.
- Resumes with quantified accomplishments are **2.3x more likely** to lead to an interview.

**Confidence level:** Strong. Large sample size, data-driven analysis of real resumes.

### Overqualification

- Employers reject overqualified candidates primarily due to **flight risk** concern — worry they'll get bored, want too much money, or leave quickly.
- **Gender asymmetry:** Overqualified men are seen as "too good for the role" and less committed; overqualified women are seen as committed and likely to stay (Organization Science study).
- The "overqualified" label often serves as **coded language for age bias**.

**Confidence level:** Moderate. Multiple studies confirm the pattern, but sample sizes vary.

## 5. Bias in CV Screening

### Racial/Ethnic Name Discrimination

#### Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) — "Are Emily and Greg More Employable?"

- **Publication:** American Economic Review, 94(4), 991-1013.
- **Sample:** ~5,000 resumes sent to 1,300+ ads in Boston and Chicago.
- **Method:** Identical resumes randomly assigned white-sounding names (Emily Walsh, Brendan Baker) or Black-sounding names (Lakisha Washington, Jamal Jones).
- **Finding:** White names received **50% more callbacks**.
- **Quality effect:** Higher-quality resumes elicited 30% more callbacks for white names but far less for Black names.
- **Scope:** Discrimination was uniform across occupation, industry, and employer size.
- **Confidence level:** Strong. Landmark study, large sample, rigorous RCT methodology. Published in top economics journal.

#### Quillian, Pager, Hexel & Midtboen (2017) — Meta-Analysis

- **Publication:** PNAS, 114(41), 10870-10875.
- **Sample:** Meta-analysis of 28 field experiments; 55,842 applications to 26,326 positions.
- **Finding:** Whites receive **36% more callbacks** than African Americans, and **24% more** than Latinos.
- **Trend:** **No improvement in discrimination against African Americans since 1989.**
- **Confidence level:** Very strong. Meta-analysis of all available field experiments, massive aggregate sample.

#### Kang, DeCelles, Tilcsik & Jun (2016) — Resume Whitening

- **Publication:** Administrative Science Quarterly, 61(3), 469-502.
- **Sample:** 1,600 entry-level job applications across 16 US metropolitan areas.
- **Finding:** Companies are **more than twice as likely** to call minority applicants who submit "whitened" (racially-scrubbed) resumes.
- **Paradox:** Organizational diversity statements did NOT reduce discrimination against unwhitened resumes. Minorities were particularly disadvantaged when applying to ostensibly pro-diversity employers.
- **Confidence level:** Strong. Published in top management journal, rigorous field experiment.

#### Oreopoulos (2011) — Canadian Name Discrimination

- **Publication:** American Economic Journal: Economic Policy, 3(4), 148-171.
- **Sample:** 6,000 resumes sent to 2,000 job postings in Greater Toronto Area.
- **Finding:** English-sounding names received **40% more interview requests** than Chinese, Indian, or Pakistani names (16% vs 11% callback rate).
- **Explanation dismissed:** Recruiters claimed concern about language skills, but signals of English fluency (listing multiple languages) did not increase callbacks for foreign-named resumes.
- **Confidence level:** Strong. Large sample, controlled field experiment.

### Age Discrimination

#### Neumark, Burn & Button (2015/2019)

- **Sample:** 40,000+ applications to 13,000+ positions across 12 US cities.
- **Method:** Fictitious resumes for young (29-31), middle-aged (49-51), and senior (64-66) applicants.
- **Finding:** Senior applicants (64-66) received **statistically significantly fewer callbacks** across all occupations and genders.
- **Gender interaction:** Women face **worse age discrimination** than men, with sharper callback drop-offs in retail sales roles.
- **Middle-aged:** Mixed results — female middle-aged applicants had significantly lower callbacks, but male middle-aged applicants did not.
- **Confidence level:** Very strong. Largest and most comprehensive age discrimination audit study to date.

**Supporting data:**
- 42% of hiring managers consider age when evaluating resumes (Resume Builder survey).
- 33% have concerns about hiring older applicants.
- 80% of workers 50+ have experienced or witnessed age bias (AARP, 2024).
- Older candidates are 11-50% more likely to be passed over.

### Gender Bias

#### Meta-Analysis of U.S. Audit Studies (Published 2025)

- **Sample:** 37 audit studies conducted between 1990-2022; aggregate sample of 243,202 fictitious applications.
- **Finding:** **No statistically significant overall gender discrimination** at the study level.
- **Nuance:** In female-dominated jobs, white female applicants receive more callbacks than males, but Black female applicants experience no such benefit.
- **Confidence level:** Strong. Large-scale meta-analysis.

### Employment Gap Bias

#### Kristal, Nicks, Gloor & Hauser (2023) — Nature Human Behaviour

- **Sample:** 9,022 resumes in preregistered field experiment (UK).
- **Finding:** Rewriting resumes to list jobs by tenure (years worked) rather than dates increased callbacks by ~8% for gapless resumes and **15% for resumes with gaps**.
- **Effect held** for both male and female applicants, and across experience levels.
- **Implication:** Employment gaps carry a measurable penalty, but formatting interventions can significantly reduce it.
- **Confidence level:** Strong. Preregistered, large-sample field experiment published in Nature Human Behaviour.

### AI-Driven Bias (Emerging)

#### University of Washington Study (2024)

- **Presented:** AAAI/ACM Conference on AI, Ethics, and Society (October 2024).
- **Sample:** 554 real resumes, 571 job descriptions, 9 occupations, 3 state-of-the-art LLMs.
- **Finding:** LLMs favored white-associated names **85% of the time**, female-associated names only 11% of the time.
- **Critical finding:** Black male-associated names were **never favored** over white male-associated names in any comparison.
- **Confidence level:** Strong. Comprehensive audit, peer-reviewed presentation.

#### Large-Scale LLM Hiring Experiment (2025, VoxDev)

- **Sample:** ~361,000 fictitious resumes with randomly assigned demographics.
- **Models tested:** 5 leading LLMs.
- **Finding:** AI models systematically favor female candidates while **disadvantaging Black male applicants**, even with identical qualifications.
- **Confidence level:** Strong. Very large sample, controlled randomized design.

**Regulatory response:**
- New York City (effective): Annual bias audits required for automated hiring tools.
- Colorado AI Act (effective June 2026): Reasonable care required to prevent algorithmic discrimination.
- California (October 2025): Anti-discrimination regulations applied to AI hiring tools.
- **Landmark case:** Mobley v. Workday — potentially covering millions of job seekers over 40.

### Application Growth

- **2.6-3x growth** in job applications observed in 2024-2025.
- Job seekers now submit **32-200+ applications** on average before receiving an offer.
- Most online applications result in a **0.1-2% success rate**.

### Ghost Jobs — A Hidden Volume Problem

- **18-27%** of online job listings are ghost jobs with no real intent to hire.
- **45% of HR professionals** "regularly" post ghost jobs; 48% do so "occasionally."
- **40% of tech companies** posted fake jobs in the past year; 79% of those listings were still active when checked.
- **30% of job postings** in June 2025 never resulted in a hire (BLS JOLTS data: 7.4M openings, only 5.2M hires).
- Ghost job rate in **corporate services: ~31%** (Q2 2024, Greenhouse).

**Implication:** A significant portion of resume "rejections" are not rejections at all — they are applications into a void. This inflates perceived failure rates and demoralizes job seekers who believe they are being evaluated when no evaluation is occurring.

**Confidence level:** Strong for existence of ghost jobs; moderate for exact percentages (self-reported surveys + estimation models).

## 9. Market Conditions

### Employer vs. Applicant Market Effects on CV Screening

**Current state (2024-2025): Cooling labor market with paradoxically high application volume.**

- **Application volume surge:** 2.6-3x growth in applications in 2024-2025.
- **Paradox:** Despite cooling, employers are not finding top talent more easily — they spend more time sifting through applications.
- **67% of recruiters** report increased time-to-hire despite more applications.
- **40% of applications** are screened out before human review.
- **83% of companies** now use AI resume screening, despite 67% acknowledging bias concerns.

### How Market Conditions Change Screening Dynamics

| Condition | Effect on CV Screening |
|-----------|----------------------|
| **Tight labor market** (applicant advantage) | Employers lower requirements, review more resumes carefully, faster callbacks |
| **Loose labor market** (employer advantage) | Stricter filtering, higher keyword thresholds, longer time-to-hire, more use of automated screening |
| **AI disruption (current)** | Mass application tools + AI screening create an arms race; volume up 2-3x; signal-to-noise ratio collapses |
| **Ghost job proliferation** | 18-27% of postings are inactive, inflating application counts and depressing response rates |

**Key dynamic:** The proliferation of AI-powered "easy apply" tools (LinkedIn Easy Apply, one-click applications, AI cover letter generators) has dramatically increased application volume. This triggers more aggressive screening (ATS filters, AI screening), which triggers more applications from job seekers trying to beat the filter — creating a negative feedback loop.

**Confidence level:** Moderate. Trend data is consistent across sources, but causal mechanisms are inferred from correlation.

## 10. Geographic and Cultural Variations

### CV Norms by Region

| Element | United States | Europe | Latin America | Asia |
|---------|--------------|--------|---------------|------|
| **Document name** | Resume | CV | CV / Hoja de vida (Colombia) | CV |
| **Length** | 1-2 pages (strict) | 2+ pages accepted | 2 pages standard | 2+ pages, detailed |
| **Photo** | Discouraged (discrimination risk) | Expected (60%+ of hiring managers), mandatory in Germany | Expected (Brazil, Colombia), prohibited in Mexico job apps, varies in Argentina | Expected (China, India) |
| **Personal info** | None (age, marital status, religion) | Varies by country | Common: marital status, age, cedula, DOB | Common: DOB, marital status, nationality, religion (India) |
| **Standard format** | Free format, tailored | Europass (EU standard, 38M+ users), but private sector prefers custom | Reverse chronological, balance personal/professional | Detailed, reverse chronological |
| **Cover letter** | Expected for most applications | Expected, often in local language | Less standardized | Varies by country |

### Europe-Specific Notes

- **Europass CV:** Developed by EU Parliament in 2004. Used by 38M+ Europeans. Standardized but criticized for lacking differentiation.
- **Private sector (especially digital/creative):** Prefers modern, tailored CVs over Europass.
- **ATS compatibility:** Europass format can sometimes confuse automated screening tools.
- **Recommendation:** Use Europass only when explicitly requested or for Erasmus+/public sector applications.

### Latin America-Specific Notes

- **Colombia:** "Hoja de vida" is the standard term. Professional photo recommended. Personal details (cedula number, marital status) still common.
- **Mexico:** Photos in job applications were prohibited to prevent discrimination, though practice varies.
- **Brazil:** Professional photo and references expected. Personal information (age still common, religion and marital status declining). Known as "curriculo."
- **Argentina:** Buenos Aires prohibits companies from requiring photos. Other regions may vary.
- **General LATAM:** Education credentials weighted heavily. Professional references standard. Two-page format normal.

### Asia-Specific Notes

- **China/Japan/South Korea:** Photos standard. Personal information extensive (DOB, marital status, nationality, sometimes blood type in Japan).
- **India:** Detailed personal information common. Education institution prestige heavily weighted.
- **Format:** Generally more detailed than US resumes, covering work history, academic background, skills, languages, personal information, and references.

**Confidence level:** Moderate. Based on industry guides and regional recruiting practice reports rather than controlled studies.

## Methodology Notes

### Source Quality Hierarchy

1. **Peer-reviewed field experiments** (Bertrand & Mullainathan, Quillian et al., Neumark et al., Kristal et al.) — Highest reliability.
2. **Large-scale data analysis** (Cultivated Culture 125K+ resumes, Deming & Noray) — High reliability for patterns.
3. **Controlled resume studies** (ResumeGo, TheLadders eye-tracking) — Moderate reliability; smaller samples but direct measurement.
4. **Industry surveys** (CareerBuilder, LinkedIn, SHRM) — Moderate reliability; self-reported data, potential response bias.
5. **Recruiter/HR platform reports** (Greenhouse, Jobscan, Ashby) — Lower reliability as primary sources; potential conflicts of interest, but useful for trend data.

### Caveats

- Many widely-cited resume statistics originate from resume-writing services or job platforms with commercial incentives.
- The "75% ATS rejection" statistic has been debunked but continues to circulate widely.
- Most eye-tracking studies have small recruiter samples (n=10-30).
- Ghost job estimates rely on self-reported HR surveys and estimation models.
- Callback rates in audit studies measure interview invitations, not hiring outcomes.
- Cultural CV norms change faster in multinational/tech contexts than in traditional sectors.
