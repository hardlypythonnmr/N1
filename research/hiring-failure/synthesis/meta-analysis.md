## 1. Schmidt & Hunter (1998) — The Foundational Meta-Analysis

### Citation
Schmidt, F. L., & Hunter, J. E. (1998). The validity and utility of selection methods in personnel psychology: Practical and theoretical implications of 85 years of research findings. *Psychological Bulletin*, 124(2), 262-274.

### What They Did
Synthesized 85 years of research in personnel selection. Presented the validity of 19 selection procedures for predicting job performance and training performance, plus the validity of paired combinations of GMA (General Mental Ability) with each of the other 18 methods.

### The Validity Table (Predicting Job Performance)

| Selection Method | Validity (r) | Category |
|---|---|---|
| Work sample tests | .54 | High |
| GMA tests (general mental ability) | .51 | High |
| Structured interviews | .51 | High |
| Peer ratings | .49 | High |
| Job knowledge tests | .48 | High |
| Job tryout procedures | .44 | Moderate-High |
| Integrity tests | .41 | Moderate-High |
| Unstructured interviews | .38 | Moderate |
| Assessment centers | .37 | Moderate |
| Biographical data (biodata) | .35 | Moderate |
| Conscientiousness tests | .31 | Moderate |
| Reference checks | .26 | Low-Moderate |
| Job experience (years) | .18 | Low |
| Training & experience ratings | .11 | Very Low |
| Years of education | .10 | Very Low |
| Interests | .10 | Very Low |
| Graphology (handwriting analysis) | .02 | None |
| Age | -.01 | None |

### Best Combinations (GMA + Second Predictor)

| Combination | Multiple Validity (R) |
|---|---|
| GMA + Integrity test | .65 |
| GMA + Work sample test | .63 |
| GMA + Structured interview | .63 |
| GMA + Conscientiousness test | .60 |
| GMA + Reference checks | .57 |
| GMA + Job experience | .54 |
| GMA + Biographical data | .58 |

### Key Implications
- **GMA is the single best predictor** of job performance across all job types, but especially for complex jobs.
- **Work sample tests** are equally valid but can only be used for experienced applicants.
- **Structured interviews** match GMA validity and add incremental validity beyond GMA.
- **Resume staples (education, experience) are among the worst predictors.** Years of education (r = .10) and job experience (r = .18) — the two things most resumes emphasize — have very low validity.
- **Graphology is useless.** This was still used in some European countries at the time.

### Confidence Level
Very high. This is one of the most cited papers in I/O Psychology (10,000+ citations). Based on decades of cumulative meta-analytic data. Published in *Psychological Bulletin*, the field's top review journal.

## 2. Schmidt, Oh & Shaffer (2016) — The 100-Year Update

### Citation
Schmidt, F. L., Oh, I.-S., & Shaffer, J. A. (2016). The validity and utility of selection methods in personnel psychology: Practical and theoretical implications of 100 years of research findings. *Working paper*, University of Iowa.

### What Changed from 1998
The 2016 update expanded the data by 15 additional years of research and applied improved statistical corrections for range restriction (using indirect range restriction corrections rather than the older direct corrections).

### Key Revisions

| Selection Method | 1998 Validity | 2016 Validity | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|
| GMA tests | .51 | .65 | Increased |
| Structured interviews | .51 | .58 | Increased |
| Unstructured interviews | .38 | .58 | Large increase |
| Work sample tests | .54 | .33 | Decreased |
| Integrity tests | .41 | .46 | Slight increase |
| Assessment centers | .37 | .36 | Stable |
| Conscientiousness | .31 | .22 | Decreased |
| Job knowledge tests | .48 | .40 | Decreased |

### Major Surprises
- **Unstructured interviews jumped from .38 to .58**, essentially equaling structured interviews. This was due to improved range restriction corrections and the finding that interrater reliability for unstructured interviews had been underestimated.
- **Work sample tests dropped from .54 to .33**, based on newer meta-analytic evidence showing earlier estimates were inflated.
- **GMA increased from .51 to .65**, becoming even more dominant as a predictor.

### Controversy
The 2016 paper was never published in a peer-reviewed journal (it remained a working paper/book chapter). The dramatic changes — especially the unstructured interview finding — generated significant debate and were later challenged by Sackett et al. (2022).

## 3. Sackett et al. (2022) — The Great Correction

### Citation
Sackett, P. R., Zhang, C., Berry, C. M., & Lievens, F. (2022). Revisiting meta-analytic estimates of validity in personnel selection: Addressing systematic overcorrection for restriction of range. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 107(12), 2040-2068.

### The Core Argument
Sackett et al. demonstrated that prior meta-analyses (including Schmidt & Hunter 1998 and Schmidt, Oh & Shaffer 2016) had **systematically inflated validity estimates** by inappropriately applying range restriction corrections developed for predictive validation studies to concurrent validation designs. Since concurrent studies don't involve selection on the predictor, applying range restriction corrections to them overcorrects and inflates estimates — sometimes substantially.

### Revised Validity Estimates

| Selection Method | Schmidt & Hunter (1998) | Schmidt et al. (2016) | Sackett et al. (2022) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Structured interviews | .51 | .58 | **.42** |
| Job knowledge tests | .48 | .40 | **.31** |
| GMA tests | .51 | .65 | **.26-.31** |
| Integrity tests | .41 | .46 | **.24** |
| Unstructured interviews | .38 | .58 | **.19** |
| Conscientiousness | .31 | .22 | **.12** |

### Key Findings
1. **Structured interviews emerged as the top-ranked predictor**, displacing GMA from its decades-long position at the top.
2. **All validity estimates dropped by .10 to .20 points** compared to prior summaries.
3. **Job-specific predictors (structured interviews, job knowledge tests, work samples, empirically-keyed biodata) outperform general psychological constructs (GMA, personality).**
4. **The "validity-diversity tradeoff" is less severe than previously believed** — GMA tests aren't as much more valid than alternatives as was thought, so using alternatives doesn't sacrifice as much validity.

### Implications
- The primacy of cognitive ability in personnel selection has been overestimated.
- Structured interviews are the best single predictor — and they also have lower adverse impact than GMA tests.
- The field's validity estimates need recalibration.

### Confidence Level
High. Published in the *Journal of Applied Psychology* (the field's top empirical journal). Uses rigorous statistical methodology. However, this is a methodological reanalysis, not new primary data — so the debate continues.

### Subsequent Response
Sackett et al. (2023) followed up with "Revisiting the design of selection systems in light of new findings regarding the validity of widely used predictors" in *Industrial and Organizational Psychology*, 16(3), 283-300, offering practical guidance for practitioners.

## 4. Structured vs. Unstructured Interviews

### Key Studies

#### Wiesner & Cronshaw (1988)
- **Citation:** Wiesner, W. H., & Cronshaw, S. F. (1988). A meta-analytic investigation of the impact of interview format and degree of structure on the validity of the employment interview. *Journal of Occupational Psychology*, 61, 275-290.
- **Sample:** 150 validity coefficients from published and unpublished literature worldwide.
- **Key Finding:** Structured interviews produced mean validity coefficients **twice as high** as unstructured interviews.
- **Additional Finding:** Interview structure is a stronger moderator of validity than interview format (individual vs. board/panel).

#### Huffcutt & Arthur (1994)
- **Citation:** Huffcutt, A. I., & Arthur, W. Jr. (1994). Hunter and Hunter (1984) revisited: Interview validity for entry-level jobs. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 79, 184-190.
- **Key Finding:** Identified four levels of interview structure and showed validity increases with each level. Corrected validity for highly structured interviews was substantially higher than for unstructured formats.
- **Moderator:** Job analysis-based questions produce higher validity.

#### Campion, Palmer & Campion (1997)
- **Citation:** Campion, M. A., Palmer, D. K., & Campion, J. E. (1997). A review of structure in the selection interview. *Personnel Psychology*, 50, 655-702.
- **Key Contribution:** Identified **15 components of structure** across two categories:
  - **Content components:** Job analysis-based questions, same questions for all candidates, limited prompting, longer interviews, controlling ancillary information, no questions from candidates until end.
  - **Evaluation components:** Rating scales with anchors, detailed notes, multiple interviewers, same interviewers for all candidates, no discussion between interviews, statistical (not clinical) combination of ratings, training for interviewers.
- **Practical Impact:** Demonstrated that organizations could selectively adopt components to incrementally improve their interviews without implementing full structure.

#### Levashina, Hartwell, Morgeson & Campion (2014)
- **Citation:** Levashina, J., Hartwell, C. J., Morgeson, F. P., & Campion, M. A. (2014). The structured employment interview: Narrative and quantitative review of the research literature. *Personnel Psychology*, 67, 241-293.
- **Key Findings:**
  - "Structured interviews are much more reliable and valid than unstructured interviews."
  - Structured interviews reduce the influence of racial, gender, and age biases on hiring decisions.
  - Situational interviews (hypothetical future behavior) and behavioral description interviews (past behavior) show comparable validity.
  - Despite 20 years of evidence, structured interviews remain underutilized in practice.

### The Adoption Gap: Why Structured Interviews Are Better But Less Used

Research consistently shows most organizations continue to prefer unstructured interviews despite evidence of their inferiority. Key reasons identified in the literature:

1. **Illusion of insight** — Managers believe unstructured conversations reveal "true" character (Highhouse, 2008).
2. **Autonomy preference** — Structured formats feel constraining; interviewers want discretion (Dana, Dawes & Peterson, 2013).
3. **Face validity perception** — Unstructured interviews feel more "natural" and fair to both parties.
4. **Trait overweighting** — When information comes from an unstructured interview rather than a test, managers assign it more importance (Lievens, Highhouse & De Corte, 2005).
5. **Lack of training** — Most hiring managers receive no formal interview training.

## 5. Resume/CV Screening Validity

### The Evidence

Resumes are the most universally used screening tool in hiring, yet they have remarkably poor predictive validity for job performance.

#### Key Findings from Schmidt & Hunter (1998)
- **Years of education:** r = .10 (very low validity)
- **Job experience (years):** r = .18 (low validity)
- **Training & experience ratings:** r = .11 (very low validity)

These are the three primary signals in a typical resume, and all fall in the lowest tier of predictive validity.

#### Cole, Rubin, Feild & Giles (2007)
- **Citation:** Cole, M. S., Rubin, R. S., Feild, H. S., & Giles, W. F. (2007). Recruiters' perceptions and use of applicant resume information: Screening the recent graduate. *Applied Psychology: An International Review*, 56(2), 319-343.
- **Finding:** Recruiters showed low inter-rater agreement on resume evaluations, with different recruiters reaching different conclusions from the same resume.

#### Resume Fabrication Problem
Research consistently shows significant levels of resume embellishment and fabrication:
- Studies estimate 30-50% of resumes contain some form of misrepresentation.
- Impression management and embellishment help explain the low predictive validity — the information itself is unreliable.

#### Why Resumes Persist
Despite poor validity, resumes persist because:
1. They are cheap and easy to collect.
2. They serve as a baseline information-gathering tool.
3. There is no widely adopted alternative for initial screening at scale.
4. Legal and organizational inertia favor traditional methods.

### Implication for N1 Content
The resume is the most labor-intensive step for job seekers and one of the least valid predictors for employers. This creates a fundamental misalignment: candidates optimize for a tool that barely predicts success, while ignoring preparation for methods (structured interviews, work samples) that actually matter.

## 6. Cognitive Ability Testing (GMA)

### The Classic Position: GMA as Top Predictor

#### Hunter & Hunter (1984)
- **Citation:** Hunter, J. E., & Hunter, R. F. (1984). Validity and utility of alternative predictors of job performance. *Psychological Bulletin*, 96, 72-98.
- **Finding:** Established GMA as the single best predictor of job performance, especially for complex jobs.

#### Schmidt & Hunter (1998)
- GMA validity: r = .51 (corrected for range restriction and measurement error).
- Validity increases with job complexity: r = .58 for high-complexity jobs, r = .23 for low-complexity jobs.
- GMA predicted both job performance and training success.

#### Schmidt et al. (2016)
- Revised GMA validity upward to r = .65 using improved range restriction corrections.

### The Revision: Sackett et al. (2022)
- Revised GMA validity **downward** to r = .26-.31 after correcting for overcorrection of range restriction.
- GMA dropped from the top predictor to third or fourth, behind structured interviews and job knowledge tests.
- However, GMA remains a meaningful predictor — it's just not as dominant as previously claimed.

### Adverse Impact Concerns

#### Sackett & Lievens (2008)
- **Citation:** Sackett, P. R., & Lievens, F. (2008). Personnel selection. *Annual Review of Psychology*, 59, 419-450.
- **Key Issue:** GMA tests consistently show adverse impact against Black and Hispanic test-takers. White-Black mean differences typically range from 0.8 to 1.0 standard deviations.
- **No Predictive Bias:** Despite mean score differences, GMA tests do not show predictive bias — they predict equally well (or slightly overpredict) for minority groups.
- **The Dilemma:** GMA tests are valid predictors but create disparate impact. This has been called the "validity-diversity tradeoff."

### Post-Sackett (2022) Implications
The revised, lower validity estimates for GMA reduce the severity of the validity-diversity tradeoff. If structured interviews are equally or more valid than GMA and produce less adverse impact, organizations have less justification for relying heavily on cognitive ability tests.

## 7. Personality Testing in Hiring

### The Big Five and Job Performance

#### Barrick & Mount (1991) — The Landmark Study
- **Citation:** Barrick, M. R., & Mount, M. K. (1991). The Big Five personality dimensions and job performance: A meta-analysis. *Personnel Psychology*, 44, 1-26.
- **Sample:** Meta-analysis across five occupational groups (professionals, police, managers, sales, skilled/semi-skilled) and three criteria (job proficiency, training proficiency, personnel data).
- **Key Findings:**
  - **Conscientiousness** showed consistent, generalizable validity across all occupational groups and all criteria (estimated true validity rho = .22-.23).
  - **Extraversion** was valid for managers and sales positions (rho = .18 for managers, .15 for sales).
  - **Openness to Experience** and **Extraversion** predicted training proficiency across occupations.
  - **Agreeableness** and **Emotional Stability** showed very small effects overall (rho < .10 for most groups).
- **Significance:** First comprehensive meta-analysis to organize personality research under the Big Five framework and demonstrate criterion-related validity.

#### Subsequent Replications and Updates
- **Salgado (1997):** Replicated Barrick & Mount in European samples. Found similar patterns — Conscientiousness and Emotional Stability were valid across occupations.
- **Hurtz & Donovan (2000):** Confirmed Conscientiousness as the only Big Five dimension with generalizable validity (rho = .20-.22).
- **Judge, Bono, Ilies & Gerhardt (2002):** Meta-analysis of Big Five and leadership. Extraversion (rho = .31) was the strongest predictor of leadership emergence and effectiveness.

### The Faking Problem

#### Birkeland, Manson, Kisamore, Brannick & Smith (2006)
- **Citation:** Birkeland, M. N., Manson, T. M., Kisamore, J. L., Brannick, M. T., & Smith, M. A. (2006). A meta-analytic investigation of job applicant faking on personality measures. *International Journal of Selection and Assessment*, 14, 317-335.
- **Sample:** Meta-analysis of studies comparing applicant vs. non-applicant personality scores.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Applicants scored significantly higher than non-applicants on:
    - **Conscientiousness:** d = .45 (moderate faking)
    - **Emotional Stability:** d = .44 (moderate faking)
    - **Extraversion:** d = .11 (slight faking)
    - **Openness:** d = .13 (slight faking)
  - Faking is **trait-specific and job-relevant** — applicants distort responses on dimensions they perceive as important for the specific job.

#### Morgeson et al. (2007) — The Provocative Critique
- **Citation:** Morgeson, F. P., Campion, M. A., Dipboye, R. L., Hollenbeck, J. R., Murphy, K., & Schmitt, N. (2007). Reconsidering the use of personality tests in personnel selection contexts. *Personnel Psychology*, 60, 683-729.
- **Authors:** Five former editors of the field's top journals.
- **Arguments:**
  1. Self-report personality tests have very low criterion-related validity (often r < .15 in practice).
  2. Faking on self-report measures cannot be eliminated.
  3. The ease of faking combined with low validity makes personality tests questionable for high-stakes selection.
- **Counterarguments (Tett & Christiansen, 2007; Ones, Dilchert, Viswesvaran & Judge, 2007):** Meta-analyses show adequate validity when tests are matched to job requirements through job analysis. The debate remains unresolved.

### Practical Summary
- Conscientiousness is the only Big Five trait with generalizable validity for job performance across occupations, but its validity is modest (r = .12-.22 depending on corrections).
- Personality tests add incremental validity beyond GMA but are vulnerable to faking.
- Self-report measures remain controversial for high-stakes selection decisions.

## 8. Integrity Tests

### Ones, Viswesvaran & Schmidt (1993)
- **Citation:** Ones, D. S., Viswesvaran, C., & Schmidt, F. L. (1993). Comprehensive meta-analysis of integrity test validities: Findings and implications for personnel selection and theories of job performance. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 78(4), 679-703.
- **Sample:** 665 validity coefficients across 576,460 data points.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Mean operational predictive validity for supervisory ratings of job performance: **r = .41**.
  - Validity for predicting counterproductive work behavior (theft, disciplinary problems, absenteeism): even higher.
  - Integrity tests primarily measure **Conscientiousness, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability** combined.
  - No adverse impact by race or gender.
- **Significance:** Demonstrated that integrity tests are among the most valid predictors of job performance, and the most valid predictor of counterproductive work behavior.

### Post-Sackett (2022) Revision
- Integrity test validity was revised downward to approximately r = .24 after correcting for overcorrection of range restriction.
- Still a meaningful predictor, especially for counterproductive behavior, but not as powerful as originally estimated.

## 9. Assessment Centers

### Gaugler, Rosenthal, Thornton & Bentson (1987)
- **Citation:** Gaugler, B. B., Rosenthal, D. B., Thornton, G. C. III, & Bentson, C. (1987). Meta-analysis of assessment center validity. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 72(3), 493-511.
- **Sample:** 50 studies containing 107 validity coefficients.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Corrected mean validity of the Overall Assessment Rating (OAR): **r = .37** for job performance.
  - Higher validities when "potential ratings" were the criterion.
  - Lower validities in promotion-focused studies.
- **Legacy:** This study remains the "gold standard" for AC validity estimation based on the OAR.

### Arthur, Day, McNelly & Edens (2003)
- **Citation:** Arthur, W. Jr., Day, E. A., McNelly, T. L., & Edens, P. S. (2003). A meta-analysis of the criterion-related validity of assessment center dimensions. *Personnel Psychology*, 56, 125-154.
- **Contribution:** Developed a taxonomy of six AC dimensions:
  1. Consideration/Awareness of Others
  2. Communication
  3. Drive
  4. Influencing Others
  5. Organizing and Planning
  6. Problem Solving
- **Validity of Problem-Solving dimension:** rho = .39.
- **Key Finding:** AC dimensions predict job performance, and the taxonomy provides a framework for comparing validity across different assessment center designs.

### The Construct Validity Debate
- Assessment centers show criterion-related validity (they predict job performance) but have been criticized for low construct validity — meaning the dimensions they claim to measure (e.g., leadership, communication) may not be what they actually capture.
- **Lievens & Conway (2001):** Found that exercise effects (performance variance across exercises) are larger than dimension effects (performance variance across dimensions), suggesting ACs measure exercise-specific behavior more than stable traits.
- **Sackett & Lievens (2008):** Well-designed ACs show better construct validity when fewer dimensions are used and when assessors are psychologists.

### Cost-Benefit Analysis
- ACs are expensive: typically $1,000-$5,000+ per candidate.
- They are time-intensive: 1-3 days for candidates.
- Despite moderate validity (r = .36-.37), their high cost makes them primarily suitable for senior/executive selection or high-stakes roles.

## 10. Bias in Hiring — Comprehensive

### 10.1 Racial Discrimination

#### Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004)
- **Citation:** Bertrand, M., & Mullainathan, S. (2004). Are Emily and Greg more employable than Lakisha and Jamal? A field experiment on labor market discrimination. *American Economic Review*, 94(4), 991-1013.
- **Method:** Sent ~5,000 fictitious resumes to 1,300+ help-wanted ads in Boston and Chicago, randomly assigning White-sounding or African-American-sounding names.
- **Key Findings:**
  - White names received **50% more callbacks** than African-American names.
  - Higher resume quality increased callbacks for White names but not for African-American names.
  - The racial gap was uniform across occupation, industry, and employer size.
  - Little evidence that employers were inferring social class from names — discrimination appeared race-based.
- **Significance:** One of the most cited studies in labor economics. Established the correspondence audit methodology as a gold standard for discrimination research.

#### Quillian, Pager, Hexel & Midtboen (2017)
- **Citation:** Quillian, L., Pager, D., Hexel, O., & Midtboen, A. H. (2017). Meta-analysis of field experiments shows no change in racial discrimination in hiring over time. *PNAS*, 114(41), 10870-10875.
- **Sample:** 28 field experiments, 55,842 applications submitted for 26,326 positions.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Since 1989, Whites receive **36% more callbacks** than African Americans and **24% more callbacks** than Latinos.
  - **No change** in the level of hiring discrimination against African Americans over the prior 25 years.
  - Modest evidence of decline in discrimination against Latinos.
- **Implications:** Despite civil rights legislation, diversity initiatives, and social change, the raw level of racial discrimination in hiring callbacks has remained stubbornly stable.

### 10.2 Age Discrimination

#### Neumark, Burn & Button (2019)
- **Citation:** Neumark, D., Burn, I., & Button, P. (2019). Is it harder for older workers to find jobs? New and improved evidence from a field experiment. *Journal of Political Economy*, 127(2), 802-837.
- **Sample:** Over 40,000 job applications.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Robust evidence of age discrimination against **older women**, especially near retirement age.
  - Considerably less evidence of discrimination against older men.
  - Each 10 years of aging reduces the callback rate by approximately **5 percentage points**.
  - Callback rate begins falling substantially for workers in their early 40s.
  - Discrimination reduces the hiring rate of workers over 40 from approximately 20% to 15% — a 25% reduction.

#### Lippens, Vermeiren & Baert (2023)
- Confirmed that hiring discrimination against older candidates is equally severe as discrimination based on race/ethnicity.
- Age discrimination is more prominent in Europe than in the United States.

### 10.3 Gender Discrimination

#### Lippens et al. (2023) and related meta-analyses
- **Citation:** Lippens, L., Vermeiren, S., & Baert, S. (2023). The state of hiring discrimination: A meta-analysis of (almost) all recent correspondence experiments. *European Economic Review*, 151, 104315.
- **Scope:** Synthesized correspondence experiments published between 2005 and 2020 across 10 discrimination grounds.

#### Key Findings from Gender Discrimination Meta-Analyses:
- **In male-dominated occupations:** Women receive ~50% fewer callbacks than men.
- **In female-dominated occupations:** Women receive ~40% more callbacks than men.
- **Pattern:** Gender discrimination follows occupational gender composition — it preserves the status quo.
- **Trend over time:** Discrimination against women for male-typed and balanced jobs has decreased over time. Discrimination against men for female-typed jobs has remained stable.
- **Intersectionality:** In female-dominated jobs, White female applicants benefit from a callback premium over males, but Black female applicants do not receive this benefit.

### 10.4 Disability Discrimination

#### Ameri, Schur, Adya, Bentley, McKay & Kruse (2018)
- **Citation:** Ameri, M., Schur, L., Adya, M., Bentley, F. S., McKay, P., & Kruse, D. (2018). The disability employment puzzle: A field experiment on employer hiring behavior. *ILR Review*, 71(2), 329-364.
- **Key Findings:**
  - Disability applications received **26% fewer** expressions of employer interest.
  - Discrimination concentrated among experienced applicants and small private companies not covered by the ADA.
  - Even for jobs where the disability should not affect productivity, disclosing an impairment reduced callback rates.

#### Wheelchair User Studies
- A field experiment found nondisabled applicants received **33% more callbacks** than similarly qualified wheelchair users for jobs where the impairment should not interfere with performance.

### 10.5 Comprehensive Discrimination Meta-Analysis

#### Lippens, Vermeiren & Baert (2023) — Full Scope
- **Ten discrimination grounds examined:** ethnicity, religion, disability, age, military service/affiliation, wealth, marital status, sexual orientation, physical appearance, gender.
- **Key Comparative Finding:** Disability, age, and physical attractiveness discrimination are equally severe as racial/ethnic discrimination.

## 11. Realistic Job Previews

### Premack & Wanous (1985)
- **Citation:** Premack, S. L., & Wanous, J. P. (1985). A meta-analysis of realistic job preview experiments. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 70, 706-719.
- **Key Finding:** RJPs have a modest negative correlation with turnover: r = -.06.
- **Mechanism:** RJPs work primarily by lowering new employees' initial job expectations.

### Phillips (1998)
- **Citation:** Phillips, J. M. (1998). Effects of realistic job previews on multiple organizational outcomes: A meta-analysis. *Academy of Management Journal*, 41, 673-690.
- **Key Finding:** Similar effect size to Premack & Wanous: r = -.06 for turnover reduction.
- **Additional Finding:** The context in which the RJP is provided affects its impact differentially on turnover and job performance.

### Earnest, Allen & Landis (2011)
- **Citation:** Earnest, D. R., Allen, D. G., & Landis, R. S. (2011). Mechanisms linking realistic job previews with turnover: A meta-analytic path analysis. *Personnel Psychology*, 64, 865-897.
- **Sample:** k = 52 studies, N ~ 17,000.
- **Key Findings:**
  - **Perceptions of organizational honesty** is the primary mechanism by which RJPs influence turnover (not met expectations, as previously believed).
  - RJPs are most strongly related to perceptions of organizational honesty (r = .11), role clarity (r = .10), and attraction (r = -.10).
  - When all five proposed mechanisms (met expectations, role clarity, honesty, self-selection, attraction) are included concurrently, only honesty significantly mediates the RJP-turnover relationship.

### Practical Assessment
- RJPs have statistically significant but practically **very small effects** on turnover.
- Their primary value may not be reducing turnover per se, but signaling organizational honesty and transparency — which has downstream effects on trust, engagement, and the employment relationship.
- Cost of implementation is low, making them worthwhile despite modest effect sizes.

## 12. Person-Organization Fit

### Kristof-Brown, Zimmerman & Johnson (2005)
- **Citation:** Kristof-Brown, A. L., Zimmerman, R. D., & Johnson, E. C. (2005). Consequences of individuals' fit at work: A meta-analysis of person-job, person-organization, person-group, and person-supervisor fit. *Personnel Psychology*, 58, 281-342.
- **Sample:** 172 studies, 836 effect sizes.

### Key Correlations

| Fit Type | Outcome | Correlation (r) |
|---|---|---|
| Person-Job (PJ) Fit | Job satisfaction | .56 |
| Person-Job (PJ) Fit | Intent to quit | -.46 |
| Person-Job (PJ) Fit | Organizational commitment | .47 |
| Person-Organization (PO) Fit | Job satisfaction | .44 |
| Person-Organization (PO) Fit | Organizational commitment | .51 |
| Person-Organization (PO) Fit | Intent to quit | -.35 |
| Person-Organization (PO) Fit | Job performance | .15 (weak) |

### Key Findings
- **PJ fit** has the strongest relationship with job satisfaction (r = .56) and performance-related outcomes.
- **PO fit** strongly predicts organizational commitment (r = .51) and turnover intentions (r = -.35).
- **Both PJ and PO fit are much better at predicting attitudes (satisfaction, commitment) than actual performance** (r = .15-.20 for performance).
- Nearly all credibility intervals excluded zero, indicating broad generalizability.

### Implications for Hiring
- PO fit assessments in hiring primarily predict whether someone will stay and be satisfied, not whether they will perform well.
- Overweighting PO fit in selection can lead to homogeneity and reduced diversity.
- PJ fit (matching skills and abilities to job requirements) is a better predictor of both satisfaction and performance.

## 13. Job Search Behavior

### Kanfer, Wanberg & Kantrowitz (2001)
- **Citation:** Kanfer, R., Wanberg, C. R., & Kantrowitz, T. M. (2001). Job search and employment: A personality-motivational analysis and meta-analytic review. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 86(5), 837-855.
- **Focus:** Meta-analysis of relationships between personality, expectations, self-regulatory variables, and job search behavior/employment outcomes.

### Key Findings

| Predictor | Correlation with Job Search Behavior | Correlation with Employment Outcomes |
|---|---|---|
| Job search self-efficacy | .27 | Significant for all 3 outcomes (offers, employment, duration) |
| Conscientiousness | .26 | Moderate |
| Extraversion | .20 | Moderate |
| Openness | .11 | Small |
| Agreeableness | .01 | None |
| Neuroticism (negative) | -.15 | Small |
| Financial need | .19 | Small |
| Social support | .12 | Small |

- All antecedent variables except optimism were significantly related to job search behavior.
- **Job search self-efficacy** was one of the few variables related to **all three** employment outcomes (number of offers, employment status, unemployment duration).
- **Job search intensity** (how hard you search) significantly predicted finding employment.

### Van Hooft, Kammeyer-Mueller, Wanberg, Kanfer & Basbug (2021)
- **Citation:** Van Hooft, E. A. J., Kammeyer-Mueller, J. D., Wanberg, C. R., Kanfer, R., & Basbug, G. (2021). Job search and employment success: A quantitative review and future research agenda. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 106(5), 674-713.
- **Key Update:** Identified **job-search self-regulation** and **job-search quality** as more promising constructs than simple search intensity. It's not just about searching harder — it's about searching smarter.
- **Framework:** Proposed organizing job search research around self-regulation processes (goal setting, planning, monitoring, adjusting).

### Implications for N1 Content
- Self-efficacy is the strongest predictor of job search success — people who believe they can find a job are more likely to find one.
- Search quality matters more than search quantity.
- A career strategy system should build self-efficacy while providing structure for self-regulation.

## 14. Recruitment Source Effectiveness

### Zottoli & Wanous (2000)
- **Citation:** Zottoli, M. A., & Wanous, J. P. (2000). Recruitment source research: Current status and future directions. *Human Resource Management Review*, 10(4), 353-382.
- **Methodology:** Quantitative review of recruitment source effectiveness across six American companies.

### Source Effectiveness Hierarchy

| Recruitment Source | Effectiveness | Turnover Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Employee referrals | **Most effective** | Lowest |
| In-house job postings | Most effective | Low |
| Re-hiring former employees | Most effective | Low |
| Walk-ins | Moderately effective | Moderate |
| Newspaper ads | **Least effective** | Highest |
| Employment agencies (gov/private) | Least effective | High |
| School placement services | Least effective | High |

### Explanatory Mechanism: The Realistic Information Hypothesis
Employees recruited through more effective sources (referrals, internal postings) may have acquired:
1. Greater quantity of information about the job and organization.
2. More accurate information.
3. More realistic information (functioning as an informal realistic job preview).

### Modern Extensions
- The same pattern has been replicated with modern recruitment sources.
- **Employee referrals** continue to produce the highest quality hires and lowest turnover.
- **Job boards** (the modern equivalent of newspaper ads) remain among the least effective sources, despite their volume.
- **Referral hires** show higher retention due to realistic job expectations and stronger cultural alignment.

### Implications
- The most common job search method for candidates (online applications to posted jobs) maps to one of the least effective recruitment sources.
- Networking and referrals — often avoided by introverts and those in career transition — produce significantly better outcomes.
- This creates a systematic disadvantage for people who lack professional networks.

## 15. The Base Rate Problem

### Taylor & Russell (1939)
- **Citation:** Taylor, H. C., & Russell, J. T. (1939). The relationship of validity coefficients to the practical effectiveness of tests in selection: Discussion and tables. *Journal of Applied Psychology*, 23, 565-578.

### Core Concept
The utility of any selection instrument depends on three factors:
1. **Validity** of the predictor (correlation with job performance).
2. **Base rate** (the proportion of applicants who would succeed without any selection).
3. **Selection ratio** (the proportion of applicants who are hired).

### How the Taylor-Russell Tables Work

The tables show the expected proportion of successful employees given different combinations of validity, base rate, and selection ratio. Nine tables correspond to base rates from .10 to .90, each showing outcomes across 21 validity coefficients (.05 to .95) and 11 selection ratios (.05 to .95).

### Key Principles

1. **When the base rate is high, selection adds little value.** If 80% of applicants would succeed anyway, even a highly valid test only marginally improves outcomes.

2. **When the selection ratio is high (must hire most applicants), selection adds little value.** If you must hire 90% of applicants, testing barely helps because you're hiring almost everyone regardless.

3. **Selection is most powerful when:** the base rate is moderate (~.50), the selection ratio is low (can be very selective), and the validity is high.

4. **Low base rates make any method look bad.** If the true base rate of "good hires" for a role is 10%, even a valid predictor produces many false positives.

### Practical Example

| Scenario | Validity | Base Rate | Selection Ratio | % Successful Hires |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| No selection | .00 | .50 | 1.00 | 50% |
| Moderate validity, moderate selectivity | .30 | .50 | .30 | 67% |
| High validity, high selectivity | .50 | .50 | .10 | 88% |
| High validity, low selectivity | .50 | .50 | .80 | 57% |
| High validity, high base rate | .50 | .80 | .30 | 92% |
| High validity, low base rate | .50 | .20 | .30 | 38% |

### Implications for the Hiring System
- In competitive labor markets with many applicants (low selection ratio), even moderately valid methods produce large improvements.
- In tight labor markets where employers must hire most applicants (high selection ratio), even the most valid methods add little value.
- For highly specialized roles with low base rates of talent, the prediction problem is inherently difficult regardless of method.
- Organizations that don't understand the base rate problem may blame their selection tools when the real issue is the applicant pool or the role definition.

## 16. Synthesis: What the Science Actually Says

### The Hierarchy of Prediction (Post-Sackett 2022)

Based on the most current meta-analytic evidence, the ranking of selection methods by predictive validity for job performance:

| Tier | Method | Best Estimate Validity |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | Structured interviews | .42 |
| **Tier 2** | Job knowledge tests | .31 |
| **Tier 2** | GMA (cognitive ability) tests | .26-.31 |
| **Tier 2** | Empirically-keyed biodata | ~.30 |
| **Tier 3** | Integrity tests | .24 |
| **Tier 3** | Work sample tests | .20-.33 |
| **Tier 3** | Assessment centers (OAR) | .36-.37 |
| **Tier 4** | Unstructured interviews | .19 |
| **Tier 4** | Conscientiousness | .12 |
| **Tier 5** | Reference checks | ~.15-.26 |
| **Tier 5** | Job experience (years) | .10-.18 |
| **Tier 5** | Education | .10 |
| **Useless** | Graphology, age | .00-.02 |

*Note: Assessment center validity (.36-.37) from Gaugler et al. (1987) has not been recalculated under Sackett et al. (2022) methodology, so its relative ranking may shift.*

### Seven Uncomfortable Truths

1. **The things candidates spend the most time on (resumes, cover letters) are among the worst predictors.** Education and experience — the backbone of every CV — have validities of .10-.18.

2. **The most valid methods are the least used.** Structured interviews and work samples are underutilized because they require more organizational investment and reduce interviewer autonomy.

3. **Discrimination in hiring has not decreased over 25 years.** Despite massive investment in DEI, field experiments show no change in racial discrimination since 1989.

4. **The best single predictor is the structured interview** — not cognitive ability, as the field believed for decades. The Sackett et al. (2022) correction shifted the entire hierarchy.

5. **Personality tests are controversial.** Conscientiousness is the only generalizable Big Five predictor, with modest validity that is further compromised by applicant faking.

6. **Networking beats everything for job seekers.** Referrals consistently produce the best hires and lowest turnover, creating a structural advantage for well-connected candidates.

7. **The base rate problem means hiring will always involve significant error.** No selection method, no matter how valid, can overcome a difficult prediction environment (low base rate, high selection ratio).

### The System Failure Map

| Stage | What Employers Do | What Science Says | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sourcing | Job boards, career sites | Referrals produce best hires | Large |
| Screening | Resume review | Education/experience are poor predictors | Massive |
| Assessment | Unstructured interviews | Structured interviews + work samples | Large |
| Decision | Gut feeling, "culture fit" | Statistical combination of valid predictors | Massive |
| Onboarding | Sink or swim | Realistic job previews reduce turnover | Moderate |
